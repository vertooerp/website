"ERP implementation" sounds like months of standstill and a blown budget. It does not have to be that way – at least not with a modular system and a lean approach. This phased plan shows how an SME can go live within four to eight weeks.

## Up front: What determines the duration

The implementation time depends primarily on two factors: the **number of modules** that should be active at launch, and the **number of source systems** from which data is migrated. Starting with two or three core modules gets you live faster than a big-bang rollout across every area.

The most important lever: **start small** and expand, rather than trying to do everything at once.

## The phased plan

### Week 1: Setup & master data

- Activate the license, provision the instance (for self-hosting, on your own or rented infrastructure).
- Define the core modules (e.g. inventory management + accounting).
- Prepare master data: export products, customers and suppliers from Excel or the legacy system.

### Weeks 2–3: Data migration & configuration

- Import master data (usually via CSV/Excel mapping).
- Set up roles and permissions, create locations.
- Configure number ranges, tax rates and payment terms.

### Weeks 3–5: Interfaces & testing

- Set up integrations (e.g. shop, dropshipping suppliers, DATEV export).
- Run test cycles with real transactions: order → shipment → invoice.
- Fine-tune based on the test results.

### Weeks 5–7: Training & pilot

- Train the team – ideally on real tasks.
- Run a pilot operation with a single area or location.
- Collect and resolve open items.

### Weeks 7–8: Go-live & expansion

- Put the core modules into production.
- Make adjustments during the first few days.
- Plan the next modules (e.g. CRM, warehouse, AI).

## Common pitfalls

- **Unclean legacy data:** Duplicates and gaps in the Excel files cost time during migration. Cleaning up beforehand pays off.
- **Too much at once:** Introducing all modules simultaneously overwhelms the team. A staggered approach is better.
- **No clear owners:** Every module needs a responsible person who makes the decisions.
