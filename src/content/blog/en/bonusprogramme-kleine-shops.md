Acquiring new customers is expensive; retaining existing ones is considerably cheaper. This is precisely where loyalty programs come in. But is the effort worthwhile for small and medium-sized shops as well? This article puts it into perspective.

## Why customer retention pays off

The rule of thumb in retail: getting an existing customer to buy again costs a fraction of what acquiring a new customer consumes. A loyalty program gives customers a concrete reason to buy from you again next time instead of from a competitor. It increases the **repeat purchase rate** and the **customer lifetime value**.

## When it pays off for small shops

A loyalty program plays to its strengths when:

- Your product range invites **repeat purchases** (consumables, accessories, fashion),
- You have a **growing base of regular customers**,
- And the process runs **automatically** — otherwise manual upkeep eats up the benefit.

It makes less sense for one-off purchases with a long repurchase cycle.

## What matters when implementing one

### 1. Simple mechanics

Customers need to understand immediately what they get. A clear "X points per euro, redeemable as a discount" beats complicated tiers.

### 2. Automatic crediting

Points should be credited **automatically** with every order — without anyone having to add them manually in the back end. This is the prerequisite for the program to pay off at all.

### 3. Tier system for frequent buyers

Levels such as Bronze, Silver, and Gold provide additional rewards for loyal customers and create an incentive to reach the next status.

### 4. Integration rather than a stand-alone solution

The program should be integrated into your ERP system and your shop — not as a separate tool that you have to maintain twice.

## Conclusion

For small shops with a repeat-purchase-friendly product range and a growing base of regular customers, a loyalty program can be an effective, low-cost retention tool — provided it runs automatically and is cleanly integrated into the shop and ERP system. For one-off purchases, the benefit is limited. What matters is not the size of the shop, but the repeat-purchase dynamics of the product range.
