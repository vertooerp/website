The cost question is usually the first one asked in an ERP project — and the hardest to answer in general terms. Because there is no such thing as "the ERP system," and the price depends on many factors. This article explains factually which cost blocks exist, which pricing models are common, and how small teams can start small — without false precision and without a sales pitch.

## Why ERP costs can't be quoted in general

An ERP system for a five-person trade business has little in common with the solution needed by a wholesaler with several locations. Scope, number of users, industry and degree of integration drive the price more than the provider does. Reliable figures therefore only exist as a range, always with the caveat "depending on requirements." Anyone who names an exact sum without knowing your needs should give you pause.

## The main cost factors

Regardless of the provider, ERP costs are made up of several building blocks. It helps to look at them separately:

- **Licence or subscription:** You either pay once for a licence (self-hosting) or a recurring monthly or annual fee (cloud/SaaS).
- **Users or seats:** Many models bill per active user. More employees in the system means higher running costs.
- **Modules and scope:** Accounting, inventory management, CRM, warehouse, production — each module expands functionality and usually the price.
- **Setup and implementation:** Configuration, data migration from legacy systems and adapting to your processes create one-off project costs.
- **Hosting:** With cloud solutions, operation is usually included. With self-hosting, server or hosting costs are added.
- **Maintenance and support:** Updates, bug fixes and a contact for problems — often billed as an annual maintenance fee or included in the subscription.
- **Training:** For the team to actually use the system, onboarding and training are a real and often underestimated item.

A common mistake is to look only at the licence or subscription fee. The one-off project costs for implementation and training can weigh just as heavily in the first year as the recurring fee.

## The two major pricing models

### Cloud SaaS subscription

You rent the software and pay on an ongoing basis — usually per user per month, often tiered by module. The barrier to entry is low, because there is no large upfront investment and operation, updates and maintenance are included. Over several years, however, subscription fees add up, and you are more tightly bound to the provider.

### Licence and self-hosting

You purchase the software (or use an open-source variant) and run it on your own or rented infrastructure. The initial investment is higher, but the ongoing per-seat fees disappear. You retain data sovereignty but take on responsibility for operation and maintenance — either internally or via a service provider.

Which model is cheaper depends on the time horizon: in the short term SaaS is often less expensive, while over many years a licence solution can pay off. You'll find a more detailed comparison in our article on self-hosting vs. cloud ERP.

## Typical orders of magnitude — read with caution

Hard numbers are deliberately quoted sparingly here, because they vary widely. As a rough orientation:

- **Small teams** with few users and standard modules often start in the low to mid three-digit range per month.
- **Implementation costs** frequently sit in the low to mid four-digit range depending on data migration and process adaptation — considerably higher for complex projects.
- **As the number of users and modules grows,** the running costs rise accordingly.

These ranges are no substitute for a quote. They only help you get a feel for the order of magnitude and avoid unrealistic expectations.

## How modular pricing helps small teams

For SMEs in particular, it is crucial not to pay for features you don't (yet) need. A modular approach lets you start with the essential building blocks and expand later as the company grows. That keeps the entry affordable, and costs grow with actual demand rather than with a rigid all-in package.

## Conclusion

What an ERP system costs is decided by your requirements, not by a price list. Watch all the cost blocks — not just the licence or subscription — factor in implementation and training, and choose a model that grows with you. A clean requirements catalogue is the best basis for comparable quotes.
