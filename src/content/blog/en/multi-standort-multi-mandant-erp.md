As soon as a company operates more than one location or several legal entities, organizing its data becomes a challenge. Two concepts help here – and are often confused: multi-location and multi-entity. This article clarifies the difference and shows what matters.

## Multi-Location vs. Multi-Entity

**Multi-location** means: *one* company with several locations (e.g. branches or warehouses in different cities). The data belongs to the same company but is filtered by location – teams, inventory, costs and orders can be viewed and managed per location.

**Multi-entity** means: *several legally separate entities* (e.g. subsidiaries of a holding company) are managed within a single system, while their data is kept cleanly separated from one another.

| | Multi-Location | Multi-Entity |
|---|---|---|
| Entity | One company | Several companies |
| Data | Shared, filtered | Separated per entity |
| Typical case | Branches, warehouses | Holding, subsidiaries |

## What Corporate Groups Should Look Out For

### 1. Clean Location Switching

Employees who look after several locations need a simple **location switcher**. Important: the data displayed (team, inventory, costs) should adjust automatically to the selected location – without anyone having to maintain data twice.

### 2. Permissions per Location

Not everyone should see every location. A good system assigns specific locations to users and only shows the ones they have been granted.

### 3. Modules per Location

Some locations need a warehouse, others only accounting. The ability to enable modules **per location** avoids cluttered interfaces.

### 4. Consolidated Reporting

Despite all this separation, management and controlling want the overall picture. Make sure that key figures can be evaluated both per location and in aggregate.

Multi-location and multi-entity solve different problems: one addresses the internal structure of a single company, the other the separation of several companies.
